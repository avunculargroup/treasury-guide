# Bitcoin Treasury — Sole Traders

> **Disclaimer:** This content is educational only and does not constitute financial, legal, or tax advice. Sole traders should consult a registered tax agent or qualified adviser before making investment decisions.

---

## Overview

As a sole trader in Australia, you operate your business as an individual — there is no separate legal entity between you and your business. This simplicity brings both advantages and considerations when it comes to holding Bitcoin as a treasury asset.

Unlike companies or trusts, a sole trader's Bitcoin holdings are ultimately personal assets, even when they are used for business purposes. The tax treatment, risk exposure, and compliance obligations all flow through to you personally.

## Why Sole Traders Consider Bitcoin

### Treasury Diversification
Many sole traders accumulate cash reserves in business bank accounts earning minimal interest. Allocating a portion of these reserves to Bitcoin can provide diversification beyond Australian dollar-denominated savings, potentially offering protection against currency devaluation over time.

### Simplicity of Execution
Compared to companies, trusts, or SMSFs, a sole trader faces fewer governance hurdles to invest in Bitcoin. There are no board resolutions, trust deed amendments, or regulatory approvals required — though proper record keeping and tax planning are still essential.

### Long-Term Savings Vehicle
For sole traders without employees, Bitcoin can serve as a form of long-term savings alongside (not instead of) superannuation. The 50% CGT discount for assets held longer than 12 months can make it tax-effective for patient holders.

## Key Tax Considerations

### Determining the Tax Treatment

The ATO distinguishes between two scenarios, and the distinction is critical:

**Scenario 1: Bitcoin as a CGT Asset (Investment/Treasury)**
If you purchase Bitcoin as a long-term holding or treasury reserve, and you do not regularly trade it, Bitcoin is treated as a CGT asset. This is the most common scenario for sole traders holding a Bitcoin treasury.

- Capital gains are calculated on disposal (selling, exchanging, or using Bitcoin to pay for goods/services)
- The 50% CGT discount applies if held for more than 12 months
- Capital losses can offset capital gains but cannot offset ordinary income
- Gains are added to your assessable income and taxed at your marginal rate

**Scenario 2: Bitcoin as Trading Stock (Business of Trading)**
If the ATO considers you to be carrying on a business of trading Bitcoin — based on factors such as frequency, volume, sophistication, and commercial intent — then Bitcoin is treated as trading stock.

- Profits are assessable as ordinary income (not capital gains)
- No 50% CGT discount is available
- Losses can be offset against other income
- Trading stock must be valued at year-end (cost, market value, or replacement value)

### How the ATO Determines Your Status

The ATO considers several factors:

| Factor | Investment | Business of Trading |
|---|---|---|
| Frequency of transactions | Infrequent | Regular and systematic |
| Holding period | Long-term (months/years) | Short-term (days/weeks) |
| Purpose | Store of value / treasury | Profit from short-term price movements |
| Volume | Small number of transactions | High volume |
| Business plan | No trading plan | Documented trading strategy |
| Time spent | Minimal | Significant time devoted |

### Bitcoin Received as Payment

If you accept Bitcoin as payment for your goods or services, the AUD value at the time of receipt is assessable as ordinary business income. This is the same as receiving payment in any other form — you must report it in your income tax return.

When you later dispose of that Bitcoin, the cost base is the AUD value at the time you received it. Any subsequent gain or loss is treated according to whether the Bitcoin is a CGT asset or trading stock.

### GST Treatment

Since 1 July 2017, the sale of digital currency is not subject to GST in Australia. This means:
- You do not charge GST when selling Bitcoin
- You cannot claim GST credits on the purchase of Bitcoin
- Bitcoin transactions should be excluded from your BAS GST calculations
- However, if you sell goods or services and accept Bitcoin as payment, the supply of goods/services is still subject to normal GST rules

### Small Business CGT Concessions

Sole traders may be eligible for small business CGT concessions under Division 152 of the ITAA 1997 if:
- Your aggregated turnover is less than $2 million, OR
- Your net CGT asset value is less than $6 million
- The Bitcoin is an active asset (used or held ready for use in the course of your business)

These concessions can include the 15-year exemption, 50% active asset reduction, retirement exemption, and rollover relief. However, whether Bitcoin qualifies as an "active asset" depends on how it is used in your business — simply holding it as an investment may not satisfy the active asset test.

## Record Keeping Requirements

The ATO requires sole traders to keep records of all Bitcoin transactions for at least 5 years. Required records include:

- **Date** of each acquisition and disposal
- **Amount in Australian dollars** at the time of each transaction
- **What the transaction was for** (purchase, sale, payment for goods/services, transfer between wallets)
- **The exchange or platform** used
- **Wallet addresses** involved in each transaction
- **Exchange receipts or confirmations**
- **Records of any Bitcoin received as payment** for goods or services, including the AUD value at the time

### Practical Record Keeping Tips

1. **Use dedicated software** — Cryptocurrency tax software (such as Koinly, CryptoTaxCalculator, or Swyftx Tax Reports) can connect to exchanges and automatically track cost bases
2. **Maintain a separate spreadsheet** as a backup record
3. **Download exchange statements** regularly — exchanges can close or lose data
4. **Screenshot or save** transaction confirmations at the time of each transaction
5. **Keep records of the exchange rate** used to convert Bitcoin to AUD for each transaction

## Custody and Security

### Personal Liability Considerations
As a sole trader, there is no corporate veil protecting your personal assets. If your Bitcoin is lost, stolen, or if an exchange fails, the loss is entirely personal. This makes security particularly important.

### Recommended Security Practices

- **Use a hardware wallet** for any significant holdings, rather than leaving Bitcoin on an exchange
- **Store seed phrases securely** in a fireproof safe or safety deposit box — never digitally
- **Use strong, unique passwords** and two-factor authentication on all exchange accounts
- **Consider geographic distribution** of backup materials
- **Keep your holdings private** — sole traders are personally identifiable, making them potential targets

### Separating Business and Personal

While you and your business are the same legal entity, maintaining clear separation in your records is essential:

- Use a dedicated exchange account for business-related Bitcoin transactions
- Keep a clear register of which Bitcoin is held for business purposes versus personal investment
- Document the business purpose for each acquisition

## Practical Steps to Get Started

1. **Consult a tax agent** — Before purchasing, understand how Bitcoin will be taxed in your specific circumstances
2. **Decide on the purpose** — Are you holding Bitcoin as a long-term treasury asset, or do you intend to actively trade? This determines the tax treatment
3. **Set an allocation limit** — Determine what percentage of your business reserves you are comfortable allocating to Bitcoin
4. **Choose an exchange** — Select an AUSTRAC-registered Australian exchange and open an account
5. **Set up secure custody** — Purchase a hardware wallet and set up proper key management
6. **Make your first purchase** — Start small to test the full workflow
7. **Set up record keeping** — Implement a system to track all transactions from day one
8. **Plan for tax** — Set aside funds to cover potential tax obligations on any gains

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not keeping records** — The single most common issue for sole traders with Bitcoin
2. **Forgetting that spending Bitcoin is a disposal event** — Using Bitcoin to pay for anything triggers a CGT event
3. **Not setting aside funds for tax** — Bitcoin gains are taxed at your marginal rate, which can be up to 47% (including Medicare levy)
4. **Mixing personal and business holdings** — This creates a record-keeping nightmare and potential tax issues
5. **Ignoring the trading stock question** — If you trade frequently, you may be classified as carrying on a business of trading, changing the tax treatment entirely
6. **Over-allocating** — As a sole trader with personal liability, risking too large a portion of business reserves in a volatile asset is particularly dangerous

> The ATO has specific guidance on cryptocurrency taxation published on its website. Sole traders should review this guidance and discuss their specific situation with a registered tax agent before investing.

## Regulatory Context

> This section provides educational information about the regulatory environment. It does not constitute legal, financial, or tax advice. Consult a registered tax agent for advice specific to your situation.

### Governing Legislation

#### Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (ITAA 1997)

The ITAA 1997 is the primary legislation governing the income tax treatment of Bitcoin for sole traders.

**Part 3-1 and Part 3-3 — Capital Gains Tax**
Bitcoin is a CGT asset under Australian tax law. For sole traders holding Bitcoin as an investment or treasury reserve:

- **CGT Event A1** — Disposal of Bitcoin (selling, exchanging, gifting, or using as payment) triggers a capital gains tax event
- **Section 110-25** — The cost base includes the purchase price plus incidental costs (exchange fees, transfer fees)
- **Section 115-25** — The 50% CGT discount applies to individuals who have held the asset for at least 12 months
- **Section 108-5** — Bitcoin is classified as a CGT asset, not as a "personal use asset" unless acquired for less than $10,000 and used primarily for personal transactions

**Division 70 — Trading Stock**
If the ATO determines the sole trader is carrying on a business of trading Bitcoin:
- Bitcoin must be brought to account as trading stock
- Section 70-35 requires trading stock on hand at year-end to be valued at cost, market value, or replacement value
- Profits are assessable as ordinary income under Section 6-5 (not as capital gains)

**Division 152 — Small Business CGT Concessions**
Sole traders may access small business CGT concessions if:
- Aggregated turnover is less than $2 million (s152-10(1AA)), OR
- Net CGT asset value is less than $6 million (s152-15)
- The asset satisfies the active asset test (s152-35 to s152-45) — the asset must be used, or held ready for use, in the course of carrying on a business

Available concessions include:
- **15-year exemption** (Subdivision 152-B) — full exemption if the asset has been held for at least 15 years
- **50% active asset reduction** (Subdivision 152-C) — reduces the capital gain by 50%, which can be applied in addition to the general 50% CGT discount
- **Retirement exemption** (Subdivision 152-D) — up to $500,000 lifetime limit
- **Rollover** (Subdivision 152-E) — defer the gain if a replacement asset is acquired

#### Income Tax Assessment Act 1936 (ITAA 1936)

**Section 6-5 — Ordinary Income**
Bitcoin received as payment for goods or services is assessable as ordinary income at the AUD market value at the time of receipt.

**Section 8-1 — General Deductions**
Expenses incurred in earning assessable income from Bitcoin (e.g., exchange fees, custody costs, accounting fees) may be deductible under the general deduction provision, provided they are not capital in nature.

#### A New Tax System (Goods and Services Tax) Act 1999

**Division 195 and Schedule 1 (Digital Currency)**
From 1 July 2017, digital currency is neither money nor a financial supply for GST purposes, but the supply of digital currency is GST-free. This means:
- Selling or exchanging Bitcoin does not attract GST
- Purchasing Bitcoin does not give rise to a GST credit
- However, if you supply goods or services in exchange for Bitcoin, the underlying supply is still subject to normal GST rules
- The GST-free treatment applies to "digital currency" as defined — a digital unit of value that functions as a medium of exchange, is not denominated in any country's currency, and is not provided by or under the control of any government body

#### Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006

While AML/CTF obligations primarily fall on digital currency exchange providers (who must register with AUSTRAC), sole traders should:
- Use only AUSTRAC-registered exchanges
- Complete all required identity verification
- Be aware that exchanges are required to report suspicious transactions and transactions over $10,000 to AUSTRAC

#### Taxation Administration Act 1953

**Section 262A — Record Keeping**
Sole traders must keep records that explain all transactions and enable their tax liability to be accurately assessed. Records must be:
- In English (or readily convertible to English)
- Retained for at least 5 years after the records are prepared, obtained, or the transactions completed
- Accessible to the ATO if requested during an audit or review

### Regulatory Bodies

#### Australian Taxation Office (ATO)

The ATO is the primary regulatory body affecting sole traders holding Bitcoin. The ATO:
- Administers income tax, CGT, and GST obligations
- Has published specific guidance on cryptocurrency taxation
- Uses data-matching programs with Australian cryptocurrency exchanges to identify taxpayers with cryptocurrency holdings
- Can issue amended assessments, penalties, and interest for non-compliance
- Conducts compliance campaigns specifically targeting cryptocurrency investors

**ATO Data Matching:**
The ATO has confirmed it collects bulk transaction data from Australian cryptocurrency exchanges. This data is matched against tax return information to identify taxpayers who may not have reported cryptocurrency gains. Sole traders should be aware that the ATO has visibility over their exchange transactions.

#### AUSTRAC

AUSTRAC regulates digital currency exchanges under the AML/CTF Act. While sole traders are not directly regulated by AUSTRAC, they should:
- Verify that their chosen exchange is registered with AUSTRAC
- Cooperate with any KYC/AML requirements imposed by the exchange
- Be aware that large or unusual transactions may be reported to AUSTRAC by the exchange

### Tax Reporting Obligations

#### Annual Income Tax Return

Sole traders report Bitcoin-related income and gains in their individual income tax return:

| Item | Where to Report |
|---|---|
| Capital gains from Bitcoin disposal | Schedule — Capital Gains Tax (CGT) |
| Bitcoin received as business income | Business income section (Individual Tax Return Supplementary) |
| Trading profits (if classified as a trader) | Business income section |
| Deductible expenses (exchange fees, etc.) | Business deductions section |
| Net capital losses carried forward | Schedule — Capital Gains Tax (CGT) |

#### BAS Reporting

- Bitcoin sales are GST-free and do not appear in the GST section of the BAS
- If you accept Bitcoin as payment for GST-liable goods or services, the GST on the underlying supply must still be reported
- PAYG instalments continue based on your overall income, which may increase if you realise Bitcoin gains

#### CGT Record Requirements

For each Bitcoin disposal, the following must be recorded and available:

1. Date of acquisition
2. Date of disposal
3. Cost base in AUD (purchase price + incidental costs)
4. Capital proceeds in AUD (sale price or market value at time of disposal)
5. Capital gain or loss calculation
6. Whether the 50% CGT discount applies (held > 12 months)
7. Any small business CGT concessions claimed

### Personal Use Asset Exemption

Bitcoin may qualify as a "personal use asset" under s108-20(2) ITAA 1997 if:
- It was acquired for less than $10,000
- It was used primarily for personal consumption (e.g., purchasing goods or services for personal use)

If Bitcoin qualifies as a personal use asset, any capital gain on disposal is disregarded. However, capital losses on personal use assets are also disregarded.

**Important:** The ATO takes a narrow view of the personal use asset exemption. Bitcoin acquired as an investment, held for an extended period, or kept on an exchange is unlikely to qualify. Sole traders holding Bitcoin as a treasury asset will almost certainly not qualify for this exemption.

### Compliance Risks and Penalties

#### Common Compliance Issues

1. **Failure to report** — Not declaring Bitcoin gains in the income tax return
2. **Incorrect classification** — Treating trading profits as capital gains (or vice versa)
3. **Inflated cost bases** — Overstating the cost base to reduce capital gains
4. **Omitting disposal events** — Not recognising that using Bitcoin to pay for something is a disposal
5. **Claiming personal use exemption incorrectly** — Bitcoin held as an investment does not qualify

#### ATO Penalty Framework

| Behaviour | Penalty (% of tax shortfall) |
|---|---|
| Failure to take reasonable care | 25% |
| Reckless behaviour | 50% |
| Intentional disregard of the law | 75% |
| Voluntary disclosure (before audit) | Reduced by 80% |
| Voluntary disclosure (during audit) | Reduced by 20% |

Interest charges (General Interest Charge — GIC) also apply on any unpaid tax from the original due date.

### Practical Compliance Checklist

#### Before Investing
- [ ] Consult a registered tax agent about the tax implications
- [ ] Determine whether Bitcoin will be a CGT asset or trading stock
- [ ] Set up a record-keeping system
- [ ] Choose an AUSTRAC-registered exchange
- [ ] Open a dedicated business bank account (if not already done)

#### Each Transaction
- [ ] Record the date, AUD amount, exchange, and purpose
- [ ] Save exchange confirmations and receipts
- [ ] Note the wallet addresses involved
- [ ] Calculate and record the cost base

#### Year-End
- [ ] Value any Bitcoin held at 30 June (if trading stock)
- [ ] Calculate capital gains and losses for the year
- [ ] Prepare records for inclusion in the income tax return
- [ ] Review whether the 50% CGT discount applies to any disposals
- [ ] Consider small business CGT concessions if applicable
- [ ] Provide all records to the tax agent for return preparation

> The ATO's cryptocurrency data-matching program means that exchange transactions are visible to the ATO. Accurate and complete reporting is essential to avoid penalties and interest charges.
