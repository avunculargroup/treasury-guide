# Custody Models

How you secure and control your Bitcoin holdings is one of the most critical decisions in a treasury strategy. Each custody model involves trade-offs between security, convenience, regulatory compliance, and operational complexity.

## Exchange Custody

**What it is:** Bitcoin held on a regulated cryptocurrency exchange (e.g., Independent Reserve, BTC Markets, Coinbase).

**Pros:**
- Simplest setup — no specialised knowledge required
- Easy to buy, sell, and report
- Regulated Australian exchanges must comply with AML/CTF obligations

**Cons:**
- Counterparty risk — if the exchange fails, your funds may be at risk (e.g., FTX collapse)
- You don't control the private keys
- Potential target for hacking

**Best for:** Organisations with smaller allocations or those in early stages of their Bitcoin journey.

## Third-Party Custodian

**What it is:** Bitcoin held by a specialised institutional custodian (e.g., BitGo, Fireblocks, Coinbase Custody).

**Pros:**
- Institutional-grade security with insurance coverage
- Regulatory compliance frameworks
- Segregated storage and independent auditing
- SOC 2 Type II compliance typical

**Cons:**
- Ongoing custody fees (typically 0.1-0.5% annually)
- Reliance on a third party
- May require minimum balance thresholds

**Best for:** Organisations with significant allocations ($500k+) or those with governance requirements mandating institutional custody.

## Self-Custody

**What it is:** Bitcoin held in a hardware wallet or cold storage controlled directly by your organisation.

**Pros:**
- Full control over private keys
- No counterparty risk
- No ongoing custody fees
- Maximum privacy

**Cons:**
- Requires technical knowledge and robust security practices
- Key loss or theft risk falls entirely on the organisation
- More complex for audit and compliance purposes
- Operational burden for key management, backup, and succession

**Best for:** Technically capable organisations with strong internal security practices.

## Multi-Signature (Multi-Sig)

**What it is:** Bitcoin held in a wallet requiring multiple private keys to authorise transactions (e.g., 2-of-3 or 3-of-5 signature schemes).

**Pros:**
- No single point of failure
- Distributed control across key holders
- Can combine self-custody with third-party key holders
- Strong governance alignment (mirrors board approval processes)

**Cons:**
- More complex setup and transaction process
- Requires coordination between key holders
- Recovery procedures must be carefully documented
- Higher technical requirements

**Best for:** Organisations prioritising security and governance, or those with board-level oversight requirements.

## Choosing a Model

Your choice should consider:
- **Allocation size** — larger holdings warrant more robust custody
- **Technical capability** — self-custody requires expertise
- **Governance requirements** — boards may mandate institutional custody
- **Risk tolerance** — balance counterparty risk vs operational risk
- **Regulatory obligations** — some entity types (e.g., SMSFs) have specific requirements
