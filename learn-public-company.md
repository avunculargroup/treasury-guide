# Bitcoin Treasury — Public Companies (ASX-Listed)

---

## Overview

ASX-listed companies operate under the most stringent regulatory framework of any Australian entity type. Adopting a Bitcoin treasury strategy requires navigating continuous disclosure obligations, shareholder expectations, institutional investor sentiment, and enhanced governance requirements.

Several ASX-listed companies have already adopted Bitcoin treasury strategies, providing precedent — but each company's circumstances are unique and must be assessed individually.

## Why Public Companies Consider Bitcoin

### Treasury Reserve Diversification
Public companies with significant cash reserves face the same inflation and low-yield challenges as private entities, but with the added pressure of shareholder expectations for capital efficiency. A modest Bitcoin allocation can diversify treasury holdings beyond traditional term deposits and money market instruments.

### Strategic Positioning
For technology and innovation-focused companies, a Bitcoin treasury can signal alignment with digital asset trends and attract a new investor base interested in Bitcoin exposure through equities.

### Precedent
Globally, companies like MicroStrategy (NASDAQ), Tesla (NASDAQ), and Block (NYSE) have adopted Bitcoin treasury strategies. In Australia, several ASX-listed companies have followed suit, creating established precedent for the approach.

## ASX Continuous Disclosure

### Listing Rule 3.1
A listed entity must immediately tell ASX once it becomes aware of any information that a reasonable person would expect to have a material effect on the price or value of its securities.

A decision to adopt a Bitcoin treasury strategy, any material Bitcoin acquisition, and significant unrealised gains or losses would likely trigger disclosure obligations.

### What Must Be Disclosed
- Board resolution to adopt a Bitcoin treasury policy
- Material Bitcoin acquisitions (the materiality threshold depends on the company's size)
- Significant unrealised gains or losses at reporting dates
- Changes to the Bitcoin treasury policy
- Security incidents involving Bitcoin holdings
- Regulatory actions or investigations related to Bitcoin holdings

### Listing Rule 3.1A Exceptions
Information need not be disclosed if:
- A reasonable person would not expect it to be disclosed
- The information is confidential and ASX has not formed the view it has ceased to be confidential
- One or more of the specified exceptions apply (e.g., incomplete proposal, trade secret)

A Bitcoin acquisition that has not yet been executed but is being considered may fall within the incomplete proposal exception — but once the board resolves to proceed, disclosure is likely required.

## Shareholder Approval Requirements

### Listing Rule 11.1
If the Bitcoin treasury strategy represents a significant change in the nature or scale of the company's activities, ASX may require shareholder approval under Listing Rule 11.1.

Whether a Bitcoin allocation constitutes a "significant change" depends on:
- The size of the allocation relative to the company's total assets
- Whether the company's core business is unrelated to digital assets
- The materiality of the Bitcoin holdings

### Listing Rule 10.1 — Substantial Assets
If Bitcoin holdings constitute a substantial asset (broadly, 5% or more of the equity interests of the company), related party transaction rules may apply to acquisitions from or disposals to related parties.

## Directors' Duties

### Section 180 — Care and Diligence
Directors must exercise the degree of care and diligence that a reasonable person would exercise in the same circumstances. For a Bitcoin treasury decision, this means:
- Commissioning independent analysis of the risks and opportunities
- Understanding the regulatory implications
- Considering the views of major shareholders and proxy advisers
- Documenting the decision-making process thoroughly

### Business Judgment Rule (s 180(2))
Directors can rely on the business judgment rule if they:
- Made the judgment in good faith for a proper purpose
- Did not have a material personal interest in the matter
- Informed themselves to the extent they reasonably believed appropriate
- Rationally believed the judgment was in the best interests of the company

Thorough documentation, independent advice, and a formal board paper are essential to rely on this protection.

### Conflict of Interest
Directors with personal cryptocurrency holdings must disclose this under s 191 (material personal interest) and may need to recuse themselves from the decision.

## Financial Reporting

### Accounting Treatment
Under Australian Accounting Standards:
- Bitcoin is typically classified as an intangible asset (AASB 138) measured at fair value
- Fair value changes are recognised in profit or loss each reporting period
- This creates P&L volatility that will be visible in half-yearly and annual reports
- Detailed note disclosures are required covering the nature of holdings, fair value methodology, and risk exposure

### Audit Considerations
The external auditor will need to:
- Verify existence and ownership of Bitcoin holdings (e.g., through custody provider confirmations)
- Assess fair value measurements
- Evaluate the adequacy of disclosures
- Consider whether a Key Audit Matter (KAM) designation is appropriate
- Assess the going concern implications if Bitcoin holdings are material and volatile

### Analyst and Investor Impact
- Equity analysts may adjust their valuation models to account for Bitcoin exposure
- ESG ratings agencies may assess the environmental impact of Bitcoin holdings
- Institutional investors with crypto-exclusion policies may reduce their positions
- Bitcoin-focused investors may increase their positions, changing the shareholder base

## Tax Considerations

### Corporate Tax
- Bitcoin gains and losses are subject to corporate tax (25% base rate or 30%)
- The 50% CGT discount is not available to companies
- Bitcoin held as a CGT asset: gains taxed on disposal
- Bitcoin held as trading stock: marked to market annually with gains/losses included in assessable income
- The tax classification depends on the company's purpose for holding Bitcoin

### Transfer Pricing (Multinational Groups)
If the public company is part of a multinational group, transfer pricing rules may apply to Bitcoin transactions between related entities in different jurisdictions.

### Franking Account
Bitcoin-related tax payments affect the franking account and the company's ability to pay franked dividends.

## Governance Framework

A public company adopting a Bitcoin treasury strategy should establish:

1. **Board-approved Bitcoin Treasury Policy** — covering allocation limits, custody requirements, risk management, and disclosure triggers
2. **Delegated Authority** — specifying who can authorise Bitcoin acquisitions and disposals, and at what thresholds
3. **Risk Management Framework** — integrating Bitcoin-specific risks into the company's existing risk register
4. **Disclosure Protocol** — clear procedures for when and how Bitcoin-related information is disclosed to ASX
5. **Custody Governance** — institutional custody with segregation, insurance, and independent verification
6. **Regular Board Reporting** — quarterly (at minimum) reporting to the board on Bitcoin holdings, unrealised gains/losses, and market developments

## Implementation Considerations

### Custody
Public companies should use institutional-grade custody:
- Regulated custodian with SOC 2 Type II certification
- Segregated accounts (not commingled with other clients' assets)
- Insurance coverage for digital asset holdings
- Independent audit and verification capabilities
- Business continuity and disaster recovery plans

### Exchange Selection
- Use AUSTRAC-registered exchanges with deep AUD/BTC liquidity
- Consider OTC desks for large transactions to minimise market impact
- Ensure the exchange can provide the documentation required for audit and disclosure

### Communication Strategy
Before announcing a Bitcoin treasury strategy:
- Brief major institutional shareholders
- Prepare a detailed ASX announcement
- Prepare FAQs for investor relations
- Brief the company's broker and analysts
- Prepare for media enquiries

## Regulatory Context

### Governing Legislation

#### Corporations Act 2001

The primary legislation governing ASX-listed companies. Key provisions:

**Chapter 2D — Directors' Duties**
- s 180: Duty of care and diligence (includes business judgment rule)
- s 181: Duty to act in good faith and for a proper purpose
- s 182: No improper use of position
- s 183: No improper use of information
- s 191: Disclosure of material personal interests

**Chapter 6CA — Continuous Disclosure**
- s 674: Listed entities must comply with continuous disclosure provisions in the listing rules
- s 674A: Criminal liability for intentional or reckless contravention
- Civil penalty provisions for failure to disclose

**Chapter 2M — Financial Reporting**
- Companies must prepare financial reports in accordance with Australian Accounting Standards
- Half-yearly and annual reporting obligations
- Directors' declaration on compliance with accounting standards

#### ASX Listing Rules

**Listing Rule 3.1 — Continuous Disclosure**
An entity must immediately tell ASX of information that a reasonable person would expect to have a material effect on the price or value of its securities. Exceptions under LR 3.1A apply in limited circumstances.

**Listing Rule 3.1B — False Market**
If ASX considers that there is or is likely to be a false market in the entity's securities, it may require disclosure even if exceptions would otherwise apply.

**Listing Rule 4.2A/4.3A — Periodic Reports**
Half-yearly and annual reports must include complete financial statements. Bitcoin holdings would be reported under the applicable accounting standard.

**Listing Rule 10.1 — Substantial Assets**
Transactions involving substantial assets with related parties require shareholder approval. If Bitcoin holdings become substantial (5%+ of equity interests), related party transactions involving Bitcoin may trigger this rule.

**Listing Rule 11.1 — Significant Change**
A listed entity must not make a significant change to the nature or scale of its activities without shareholder approval. Whether a Bitcoin treasury constitutes a "significant change" depends on its materiality relative to the company.

**Listing Rule 12.2 — Appropriate Structure**
ASX may form the view that the entity's operations or structure are not appropriate for a listed entity. This is unlikely for a modest Bitcoin treasury allocation but could be relevant if Bitcoin becomes the dominant asset.

#### Australian Securities and Investments Commission Act 2001

ASIC has broad powers to investigate and take enforcement action for:
- Market misconduct (insider trading, market manipulation)
- Misleading or deceptive conduct in relation to financial products
- Failure to comply with continuous disclosure obligations
- Breach of directors' duties

#### Income Tax Assessment Act 1997

- Division 104: CGT events triggered by Bitcoin disposals
- Division 70: Trading stock provisions (if Bitcoin is held as trading stock)
- Division 820: Thin capitalisation rules (for multinational groups)
- Division 815: Transfer pricing rules (for related party cross-border transactions)

### Regulatory Bodies

#### ASIC
ASIC regulates public companies and the securities market. Relevant functions:
- Monitoring continuous disclosure compliance
- Investigating breaches of directors' duties
- Reviewing financial reports for compliance with accounting standards
- Taking enforcement action for market misconduct
- Issuing regulatory guides and class orders

#### ASX
ASX operates the securities market and administers the Listing Rules:
- Monitoring compliance with continuous disclosure obligations
- Issuing price queries when unusual trading activity occurs
- Reviewing material announcements before release
- Granting or withholding trading halts for material announcements

#### ATO
The ATO administers tax obligations:
- Corporate income tax on Bitcoin gains
- Franking account implications
- Transfer pricing compliance for multinational groups
- Activity statement obligations

### Accounting Standards

#### AASB 138 — Intangible Assets
Bitcoin is typically classified as an intangible asset with an indefinite useful life. Key requirements:
- Initial recognition at cost (purchase price plus directly attributable costs)
- Subsequent measurement at fair value (if revaluation model is adopted) or cost less impairment
- Fair value changes recognised in other comprehensive income (revaluation model) or impairment recognised in profit or loss (cost model)

#### AASB 13 — Fair Value Measurement
Fair value of Bitcoin is determined using:
- Level 1 inputs: Quoted prices in active markets (exchange prices)
- The fair value hierarchy requires disclosure of the level of inputs used
- For large holdings, consideration of whether the quoted price reflects the price achievable for the full position

#### AASB 7 / AASB 132 — Financial Instruments
If Bitcoin is classified as a financial asset (less common but possible under some interpretations):
- Detailed risk disclosures are required
- Sensitivity analysis for market risk
- Liquidity risk disclosures

#### AASB 101 — Presentation of Financial Statements
- Bitcoin holdings should be separately disclosed if material
- Classification as current or non-current depends on the entity's intention (hold >12 months = non-current)
- Significant accounting policy disclosures required

### Compliance Checklist for Public Companies

#### Pre-Decision
- [ ] Commission independent analysis of Bitcoin treasury risks and opportunities
- [ ] Obtain legal advice on ASX disclosure obligations
- [ ] Assess whether the strategy constitutes a "significant change" under LR 11.1
- [ ] Identify and manage director conflicts of interest
- [ ] Brief the company's external auditor
- [ ] Brief the company secretary on disclosure requirements

#### Board Approval
- [ ] Prepare a comprehensive board paper covering rationale, risks, allocation, custody, and governance
- [ ] Obtain board approval via formal resolution
- [ ] Record detailed minutes of the discussion and decision
- [ ] Establish delegated authorities for Bitcoin transactions

#### Disclosure
- [ ] Prepare ASX announcement (consult with ASX Listings if uncertain about disclosure requirements)
- [ ] Request trading halt if necessary during announcement preparation
- [ ] Brief major shareholders, broker, and analysts
- [ ] Prepare investor relations materials and FAQs
- [ ] Update the company's website with relevant information

#### Implementation
- [ ] Engage an institutional custody provider
- [ ] Open accounts with AUSTRAC-registered exchanges
- [ ] Establish internal controls and approval workflows
- [ ] Set up accounting processes for fair value measurement
- [ ] Update the risk management framework
- [ ] Brief the external auditor on custody arrangements and verification procedures

#### Ongoing
- [ ] Disclose material Bitcoin transactions to ASX as they occur
- [ ] Include Bitcoin holdings in half-yearly and annual financial reports
- [ ] Report to the board at least quarterly on Bitcoin holdings and unrealised gains/losses
- [ ] Review the Bitcoin treasury policy annually
- [ ] Monitor regulatory developments from ASIC, ASX, and ATO
- [ ] Assess shareholder sentiment at AGM and through ongoing engagement
