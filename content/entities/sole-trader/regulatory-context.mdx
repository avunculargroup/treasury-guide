## Governing Legislation

### Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (ITAA 1997)

The ITAA 1997 is the primary legislation governing the income tax treatment of Bitcoin for sole traders.

**Part 3-1 and Part 3-3 — Capital Gains Tax**
Bitcoin is a CGT asset under Australian tax law. For sole traders holding Bitcoin as an investment or treasury reserve:

- **CGT Event A1** — Disposal of Bitcoin (selling, exchanging, gifting, or using as payment) triggers a capital gains tax event
- **Section 110-25** — The cost base includes the purchase price plus incidental costs (exchange fees, transfer fees)
- **Section 115-25** — The 50% CGT discount applies to individuals who have held the asset for at least 12 months
- **Section 108-5** — Bitcoin is classified as a CGT asset, not as a "personal use asset" unless acquired for less than $10,000 and used primarily for personal transactions

**Division 70 — Trading Stock**
If the ATO determines the sole trader is carrying on a business of trading Bitcoin:
- Bitcoin must be brought to account as trading stock
- Section 70-35 requires trading stock on hand at year-end to be valued at cost, market value, or replacement value
- Profits are assessable as ordinary income under Section 6-5 (not as capital gains)

**Division 152 — Small Business CGT Concessions**
Sole traders may access small business CGT concessions if:
- Aggregated turnover is less than $2 million (s152-10(1AA)), OR
- Net CGT asset value is less than $6 million (s152-15)
- The asset satisfies the active asset test (s152-35 to s152-45) — the asset must be used, or held ready for use, in the course of carrying on a business

Available concessions include:
- **15-year exemption** (Subdivision 152-B) — full exemption if the asset has been held for at least 15 years
- **50% active asset reduction** (Subdivision 152-C) — reduces the capital gain by 50%, which can be applied in addition to the general 50% CGT discount
- **Retirement exemption** (Subdivision 152-D) — up to $500,000 lifetime limit
- **Rollover** (Subdivision 152-E) — defer the gain if a replacement asset is acquired

### Income Tax Assessment Act 1936 (ITAA 1936)

**Section 6-5 — Ordinary Income**
Bitcoin received as payment for goods or services is assessable as ordinary income at the AUD market value at the time of receipt.

**Section 8-1 — General Deductions**
Expenses incurred in earning assessable income from Bitcoin (e.g., exchange fees, custody costs, accounting fees) may be deductible under the general deduction provision, provided they are not capital in nature.

### A New Tax System (Goods and Services Tax) Act 1999

**Division 195 and Schedule 1 (Digital Currency)**
From 1 July 2017, digital currency is neither money nor a financial supply for GST purposes, but the supply of digital currency is GST-free. This means:
- Selling or exchanging Bitcoin does not attract GST
- Purchasing Bitcoin does not give rise to a GST credit
- However, if you supply goods or services in exchange for Bitcoin, the underlying supply is still subject to normal GST rules
- The GST-free treatment applies to "digital currency" as defined — a digital unit of value that functions as a medium of exchange, is not denominated in any country's currency, and is not provided by or under the control of any government body

### Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006

While AML/CTF obligations primarily fall on digital currency exchange providers (who must register with AUSTRAC), sole traders should:
- Use only AUSTRAC-registered exchanges
- Complete all required identity verification
- Be aware that exchanges are required to report suspicious transactions and transactions over $10,000 to AUSTRAC

### Taxation Administration Act 1953

**Section 262A — Record Keeping**
Sole traders must keep records that explain all transactions and enable their tax liability to be accurately assessed. Records must be:
- In English (or readily convertible to English)
- Retained for at least 5 years after the records are prepared, obtained, or the transactions completed
- Accessible to the ATO if requested during an audit or review

## Regulatory Bodies

### Australian Taxation Office (ATO)

The ATO is the primary regulatory body affecting sole traders holding Bitcoin. The ATO:
- Administers income tax, CGT, and GST obligations
- Has published specific guidance on cryptocurrency taxation
- Uses data-matching programs with Australian cryptocurrency exchanges to identify taxpayers with cryptocurrency holdings
- Can issue amended assessments, penalties, and interest for non-compliance
- Conducts compliance campaigns specifically targeting cryptocurrency investors

**ATO Data Matching:**
The ATO has confirmed it collects bulk transaction data from Australian cryptocurrency exchanges. This data is matched against tax return information to identify taxpayers who may not have reported cryptocurrency gains. Sole traders should be aware that the ATO has visibility over their exchange transactions.

### AUSTRAC

AUSTRAC regulates digital currency exchanges under the AML/CTF Act. While sole traders are not directly regulated by AUSTRAC, they should:
- Verify that their chosen exchange is registered with AUSTRAC
- Cooperate with any KYC/AML requirements imposed by the exchange
- Be aware that large or unusual transactions may be reported to AUSTRAC by the exchange

## Tax Reporting Obligations

### Annual Income Tax Return

Sole traders report Bitcoin-related income and gains in their individual income tax return:

| Item | Where to Report |
|---|---|
| Capital gains from Bitcoin disposal | Schedule — Capital Gains Tax (CGT) |
| Bitcoin received as business income | Business income section (Individual Tax Return Supplementary) |
| Trading profits (if classified as a trader) | Business income section |
| Deductible expenses (exchange fees, etc.) | Business deductions section |
| Net capital losses carried forward | Schedule — Capital Gains Tax (CGT) |

### BAS Reporting

- Bitcoin sales are GST-free and do not appear in the GST section of the BAS
- If you accept Bitcoin as payment for GST-liable goods or services, the GST on the underlying supply must still be reported
- PAYG instalments continue based on your overall income, which may increase if you realise Bitcoin gains

### CGT Record Requirements

For each Bitcoin disposal, the following must be recorded and available:

1. Date of acquisition
2. Date of disposal
3. Cost base in AUD (purchase price + incidental costs)
4. Capital proceeds in AUD (sale price or market value at time of disposal)
5. Capital gain or loss calculation
6. Whether the 50% CGT discount applies (held > 12 months)
7. Any small business CGT concessions claimed

## Personal Use Asset Exemption

Bitcoin may qualify as a "personal use asset" under s108-20(2) ITAA 1997 if:
- It was acquired for less than $10,000
- It was used primarily for personal consumption (e.g., purchasing goods or services for personal use)

If Bitcoin qualifies as a personal use asset, any capital gain on disposal is disregarded. However, capital losses on personal use assets are also disregarded.

**Important:** The ATO takes a narrow view of the personal use asset exemption. Bitcoin acquired as an investment, held for an extended period, or kept on an exchange is unlikely to qualify. Sole traders holding Bitcoin as a treasury asset will almost certainly not qualify for this exemption.

## Compliance Risks and Penalties

### Common Compliance Issues

1. **Failure to report** — Not declaring Bitcoin gains in the income tax return
2. **Incorrect classification** — Treating trading profits as capital gains (or vice versa)
3. **Inflated cost bases** — Overstating the cost base to reduce capital gains
4. **Omitting disposal events** — Not recognising that using Bitcoin to pay for something is a disposal
5. **Claiming personal use exemption incorrectly** — Bitcoin held as an investment does not qualify

### ATO Penalty Framework

| Behaviour | Penalty (% of tax shortfall) |
|---|---|
| Failure to take reasonable care | 25% |
| Reckless behaviour | 50% |
| Intentional disregard of the law | 75% |
| Voluntary disclosure (before audit) | Reduced by 80% |
| Voluntary disclosure (during audit) | Reduced by 20% |

Interest charges (General Interest Charge — GIC) also apply on any unpaid tax from the original due date.

## Practical Compliance Checklist

### Before Investing
- [ ] Consult a registered tax agent about the tax implications
- [ ] Determine whether Bitcoin will be a CGT asset or trading stock
- [ ] Set up a record-keeping system
- [ ] Choose an AUSTRAC-registered exchange
- [ ] Open a dedicated business bank account (if not already done)

### Each Transaction
- [ ] Record the date, AUD amount, exchange, and purpose
- [ ] Save exchange confirmations and receipts
- [ ] Note the wallet addresses involved
- [ ] Calculate and record the cost base

### Year-End
- [ ] Value any Bitcoin held at 30 June (if trading stock)
- [ ] Calculate capital gains and losses for the year
- [ ] Prepare records for inclusion in the income tax return
- [ ] Review whether the 50% CGT discount applies to any disposals
- [ ] Consider small business CGT concessions if applicable
- [ ] Provide all records to the tax agent for return preparation

<Callout type="info">
The ATO's cryptocurrency data-matching program means that exchange transactions are visible to the ATO. Accurate and complete reporting is essential to avoid penalties and interest charges.
</Callout>
