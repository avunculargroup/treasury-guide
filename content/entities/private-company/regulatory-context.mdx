## Governing Legislation

### Corporations Act 2001

The Corporations Act is the primary legislation governing the operation of private (proprietary limited) companies in Australia.

**Part 2D.1 — Directors' Duties**

- **Section 180 — Care and diligence**: Directors must exercise their powers with the degree of care and diligence that a reasonable person would exercise if they were a director of the company in the company's circumstances and occupied the same office. The business judgment rule (s180(2)) provides a safe harbour for informed, good faith decisions.

- **Section 181 — Good faith and proper purpose**: Directors must exercise their powers in good faith in the best interests of the company and for a proper purpose. A Bitcoin investment must serve the company's interests, not the personal interests of directors.

- **Section 182 — No improper use of position**: Directors must not improperly use their position to gain a personal advantage or cause detriment to the company.

- **Section 183 — No improper use of information**: Directors must not improperly use information obtained through their position.

- **Section 184 — Criminal offence for reckless or intentional breach**: Reckless or intentional breaches of duties carry criminal penalties.

- **Section 191 — Disclosure of material personal interests**: Directors must give the other directors notice of any material personal interest in a matter relating to the company's affairs. A director who personally holds Bitcoin has a material interest in a decision about the company investing in Bitcoin.

- **Section 195 — Restriction on voting**: In a proprietary company with more than one director, a director with a material personal interest in a matter may be prohibited from voting on the matter (subject to certain exceptions).

**Part 5.7B — Insolvent Trading**

- **Section 588G — Director's duty to prevent insolvent trading**: A director must not allow the company to incur a debt when there are reasonable grounds to suspect that the company is insolvent or would become insolvent. A large Bitcoin allocation that declines significantly could contribute to insolvency if the company cannot meet its debts.

- **Section 588GA — Safe harbour protection**: Directors may be protected from insolvent trading liability if they are taking a course of action reasonably likely to lead to a better outcome for the company. This protection requires active steps and documentation.

**Section 286 — Record Keeping**
The company must keep written financial records that correctly record and explain the company's transactions and financial position. This includes Bitcoin transactions and holdings.

**Section 292-294 — Financial Reporting**
Large proprietary companies must prepare financial reports in accordance with Australian Accounting Standards and have them audited. Even small proprietary companies must maintain adequate financial records.

### Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (ITAA 1997)

**Part 3-1 and Part 3-3 — Capital Gains Tax**

- Bitcoin is a CGT asset for companies
- CGT Event A1 is triggered on disposal (sale, exchange, or use as payment)
- The 50% CGT discount is NOT available to companies (s115-25(1))
- Capital gains are included in full in the company's assessable income
- Capital losses can offset capital gains but cannot offset ordinary income
- Unused capital losses carry forward to future income years

**Section 110-25 — Cost Base**
The cost base of Bitcoin includes:
- First element: purchase price (money or property given to acquire the Bitcoin)
- Second element: incidental costs (exchange fees, brokerage)
- The cost base is calculated in Australian dollars

**Division 70 — Trading Stock**
If the company carries on a business of Bitcoin trading:
- Bitcoin is treated as trading stock
- Trading stock on hand at year-end must be valued at cost, market value, or replacement value
- Changes in trading stock value are taken into account in calculating assessable income

**Division 152 — Small Business CGT Concessions**
Note: These concessions apply at the shareholder level when shares are sold, not at the company level when the company sells Bitcoin. However, the company's Bitcoin holdings affect the maximum net asset value test ($6 million) for shareholders seeking to apply the concessions when disposing of their shares.

### Income Tax Assessment Act 1936 (ITAA 1936)

**Division 7A — Deemed Dividends**
Division 7A is critical for private companies. It applies when a private company provides a benefit to a shareholder or associate of a shareholder:

- **Section 109C — Payments**: A payment of Bitcoin from the company to a shareholder is a deemed dividend (unless it is a normal commercial transaction or a distribution already treated as a dividend)
- **Section 109D — Loans**: If Bitcoin is loaned to a shareholder, it is a deemed dividend unless a Division 7A compliant loan agreement is in place
- **Section 109E — Forgiven debts**: If a debt owed by a shareholder (including for Bitcoin received) is forgiven, the forgiven amount is a deemed dividend

A deemed dividend under Division 7A is unfranked, meaning the shareholder receives no franking credit and the full amount is taxed at the shareholder's marginal rate.

**Prevention:** Ensure that no Bitcoin is transferred, loaned, or made available to shareholders or associates without proper consideration and documentation.

**Part IVA — General Anti-Avoidance**
Part IVA allows the ATO to cancel tax benefits arising from schemes entered into for the dominant purpose of obtaining a tax benefit. Bitcoin arrangements that are primarily structured for tax advantages (rather than genuine commercial reasons) may be challenged.

### A New Tax System (Goods and Services Tax) Act 1999

- Sales of digital currency are GST-free
- Purchases of Bitcoin do not give rise to GST input tax credits
- Supplies of goods or services in exchange for Bitcoin are subject to normal GST rules
- The company's BAS should exclude Bitcoin transactions from GST calculations

### Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006

- Digital currency exchanges must be registered with AUSTRAC
- The company should use only AUSTRAC-registered exchanges
- KYC/AML verification must be completed in the company's name
- The company's exchange transactions may be reported to AUSTRAC by the exchange

## Regulatory Bodies

### Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC)

ASIC is the primary corporate regulator. Its role relevant to private companies holding Bitcoin:

- Administers the Corporations Act, including directors' duties
- Maintains the company register
- Can investigate and prosecute breaches of directors' duties
- Receives and reviews financial reports from large proprietary companies
- Issues regulatory guidance on corporate governance

**ASIC Regulatory Guide 76 — Related Party Transactions**
While primarily aimed at public companies, the principles of proper disclosure and fairness in related-party transactions are relevant to private companies. Bitcoin transactions between the company and directors or related parties should be conducted on arm's length terms.

### Australian Taxation Office (ATO)

The ATO administers:
- Company income tax (including CGT on Bitcoin disposals)
- Division 7A compliance
- GST obligations
- PAYG and franking account obligations
- Data matching with cryptocurrency exchanges

**ATO Compliance Focus Areas:**
- The ATO uses data matching with exchanges to identify companies that hold or transact in cryptocurrency
- Division 7A compliance is a key audit focus for private companies
- Companies claiming Bitcoin as a CGT asset (rather than trading stock) may be scrutinised if trading activity is high

## Franking Account Implications

### How Franking Works with Bitcoin

When a company pays tax on Bitcoin gains, the tax payment creates franking credits:

1. Company realises a capital gain on Bitcoin
2. The gain is included in assessable income and taxed at the company rate
3. The tax paid is credited to the company's franking account
4. When the company pays dividends, it can attach franking credits (franked dividends)
5. Shareholders include the grossed-up dividend in their assessable income and receive a tax offset for the franking credits

### Franking Considerations

- **Sufficient franking credits**: The company can only frank dividends to the extent it has franking credits available
- **Maximum franking rate**: Dividends must be franked at a consistent rate within the same franking period
- **Benchmark rule**: The first frankable distribution in a franking period sets the benchmark for subsequent distributions
- **Franking deficit tax**: If the franking account goes into deficit at year-end, a franking deficit tax is payable

## Shareholder Considerations

### Informing Shareholders

While private companies are not required to make public announcements, good governance suggests:

- Informing shareholders about material treasury decisions, especially if the Bitcoin allocation is significant relative to the company's total assets
- Including Bitcoin holdings in any shareholder financial reports
- Being prepared to explain the investment rationale to shareholders

### Shareholder Disputes

If shareholders disagree with the Bitcoin investment decision:
- Minority shareholders may apply to the court under s232-233 Corporations Act (oppression remedies) if the investment is oppressive, unfairly prejudicial, or unfairly discriminatory
- Shareholders can requisition a general meeting to discuss the investment
- 5% of shareholders can request the company prepare financial statements

### Share Valuation Impact

Bitcoin holdings affect the value of the company's shares. This is relevant for:
- Share transfers and buybacks
- Capital gains calculations when shares are sold
- Division 7A considerations (shareholder loans secured by shares)
- Small business CGT concession eligibility (net asset value test)

## Compliance Checklist

### Before Investing
- [ ] Review the company constitution for investment powers
- [ ] Directors disclose material personal interests (s191)
- [ ] Obtain professional legal and tax advice
- [ ] Pass a board resolution with proper minutes
- [ ] Consider shareholder notification
- [ ] Update the company's investment policy (if one exists)

### Transaction Level
- [ ] Use an exchange account in the company's name
- [ ] Record all transactions with date, AUD value, exchange, and purpose
- [ ] Ensure no Division 7A issues (no benefits to shareholders)
- [ ] Maintain arm's length terms for any related-party transactions

### Ongoing
- [ ] Monitor the company's solvency position
- [ ] Include Bitcoin in financial records and reports
- [ ] Calculate and report capital gains at year-end
- [ ] Maintain the franking account accurately
- [ ] Review the investment at least annually
- [ ] Keep records for at least 5 years (7 years recommended)

### Year-End
- [ ] Value Bitcoin holdings at 30 June
- [ ] Calculate capital gains or losses on disposals during the year
- [ ] If trading stock, value at cost, market value, or replacement value
- [ ] Include in the company tax return
- [ ] Update the franking account for tax paid
- [ ] Prepare financial statements (if required or elected)

<Callout type="info">
The intersection of the Corporations Act, income tax law, and Division 7A creates a complex compliance environment for private companies holding Bitcoin. Professional advice from a solicitor and tax agent with relevant experience is strongly recommended.
</Callout>
