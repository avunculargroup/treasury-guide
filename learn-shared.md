## What Is a Bitcoin Treasury?

A Bitcoin treasury refers to an organisation holding Bitcoin (BTC) as part of its financial reserves, alongside or in place of traditional assets like cash, term deposits, or government bonds.

### The Global Movement

Since MicroStrategy's landmark decision in August 2020 to convert its cash reserves into Bitcoin, a growing number of organisations globally have adopted Bitcoin treasury strategies. The thesis is straightforward: holding a portion of reserves in a scarce, decentralised digital asset can serve as a long-term store of value.

Notable examples include:
- **MicroStrategy** — Holds over 200,000 BTC as its primary treasury reserve asset
- **Tesla** — Added Bitcoin to its balance sheet in 2021
- **Block (formerly Square)** — Allocated a percentage of total assets to Bitcoin
- **Numerous smaller companies** — From tech firms to mining companies worldwide

### How It Works

A Bitcoin treasury strategy typically involves:

1. **Board or governance approval** to allocate a percentage of reserves
2. **Establishing a custody solution** to securely hold the Bitcoin
3. **Acquiring Bitcoin** through an exchange or OTC desk
4. **Accounting and reporting** the holdings according to applicable standards
5. **Ongoing management** including rebalancing, security reviews, and compliance

### Key Distinction

A Bitcoin treasury is **not** the same as speculative crypto trading. It is a deliberate, policy-driven allocation of organisational reserves into Bitcoin as a long-term strategic asset. The approach requires proper governance, compliance, and risk management — especially in the Australian regulatory context.

## Why Organisations Hold Bitcoin

Organisations adopt Bitcoin treasury strategies for several interconnected reasons. Understanding these motivations helps assess whether a Bitcoin allocation aligns with your entity's goals.

### Inflation Hedge

Traditional cash reserves lose purchasing power over time due to inflation. Bitcoin's fixed supply of 21 million coins creates scarcity that many view as a hedge against monetary debasement. The Reserve Bank of Australia's monetary policy decisions directly impact the purchasing power of AUD-denominated reserves.

### Diversification

Bitcoin has historically shown low correlation with traditional asset classes like equities and bonds. Adding a small Bitcoin allocation to a treasury portfolio can improve risk-adjusted returns through genuine diversification — though past performance does not guarantee future results.

### Store of Value

Bitcoin's properties — scarcity, portability, divisibility, durability, and censorship resistance — give it characteristics often compared to gold. Unlike gold, Bitcoin can be transferred globally in minutes and verified independently.

### Brand Signal & Stakeholder Alignment

For some organisations, holding Bitcoin signals innovation, forward-thinking leadership, and alignment with a growing community of investors and customers who value digital asset adoption.

### Treasury Yield Enhancement

In low-interest-rate environments, traditional cash management (term deposits, money market funds) generates minimal returns. While Bitcoin is more volatile, its long-term appreciation potential can enhance overall treasury yields for organisations with appropriate risk tolerance and time horizons.

### Important Considerations

- Bitcoin is **highly volatile** — drawdowns of 50-80% have occurred historically
- The Australian dollar exchange rate adds another layer of currency risk
- Regulatory treatment in Australia continues to evolve
- Not every organisation's risk profile is suited to Bitcoin exposure
- This is **not** a recommendation — proper assessment is essential

## Custody Models

How you secure and control your Bitcoin holdings is one of the most critical decisions in a treasury strategy. Each custody model involves trade-offs between security, convenience, regulatory compliance, and operational complexity.

### Exchange Custody

**What it is:** Bitcoin held on a regulated cryptocurrency exchange (e.g., Independent Reserve, BTC Markets, Coinbase).

**Pros:**
- Simplest setup — no specialised knowledge required
- Easy to buy, sell, and report
- Regulated Australian exchanges must comply with AML/CTF obligations

**Cons:**
- Counterparty risk — if the exchange fails, your funds may be at risk (e.g., FTX collapse)
- You don't control the private keys
- Potential target for hacking

**Best for:** Organisations with smaller allocations or those in early stages of their Bitcoin journey.

### Third-Party Custodian

**What it is:** Bitcoin held by a specialised institutional custodian (e.g., BitGo, Fireblocks, Coinbase Custody).

**Pros:**
- Institutional-grade security with insurance coverage
- Regulatory compliance frameworks
- Segregated storage and independent auditing
- SOC 2 Type II compliance typical

**Cons:**
- Ongoing custody fees (typically 0.1-0.5% annually)
- Reliance on a third party
- May require minimum balance thresholds

**Best for:** Organisations with significant allocations ($500k+) or those with governance requirements mandating institutional custody.

### Self-Custody

**What it is:** Bitcoin held in a hardware wallet or cold storage controlled directly by your organisation.

**Pros:**
- Full control over private keys
- No counterparty risk
- No ongoing custody fees
- Maximum privacy

**Cons:**
- Requires technical knowledge and robust security practices
- Key loss or theft risk falls entirely on the organisation
- More complex for audit and compliance purposes
- Operational burden for key management, backup, and succession

**Best for:** Technically capable organisations with strong internal security practices.

### Multi-Signature (Multi-Sig)

**What it is:** Bitcoin held in a wallet requiring multiple private keys to authorise transactions (e.g., 2-of-3 or 3-of-5 signature schemes).

**Pros:**
- No single point of failure
- Distributed control across key holders
- Can combine self-custody with third-party key holders
- Strong governance alignment (mirrors board approval processes)

**Cons:**
- More complex setup and transaction process
- Requires coordination between key holders
- Recovery procedures must be carefully documented
- Higher technical requirements

**Best for:** Organisations prioritising security and governance, or those with board-level oversight requirements.

### Choosing a Model

Your choice should consider:
- **Allocation size** — larger holdings warrant more robust custody
- **Technical capability** — self-custody requires expertise
- **Governance requirements** — boards may mandate institutional custody
- **Risk tolerance** — balance counterparty risk vs operational risk
- **Regulatory obligations** — some entity types (e.g., SMSFs) have specific requirements

## Risks of a Bitcoin Treasury

Understanding and managing risk is fundamental to any Bitcoin treasury strategy. Each risk must be assessed in the context of your entity type, size, and regulatory obligations.

### Volatility Risk

Bitcoin is significantly more volatile than traditional treasury assets. Historical drawdowns of 50-80% from all-time highs have occurred in every market cycle. Your organisation must be prepared for:
- Material mark-to-market losses on financial statements
- Stakeholder concern during price declines
- The possibility that the investment takes years to recover

**Mitigation:** Size the allocation appropriately, use dollar-cost averaging (DCA), maintain adequate AUD liquidity buffers, and set a long investment horizon.

### Custody and Security Risk

Digital assets can be permanently lost through:
- Loss of private keys or seed phrases
- Hardware failure without adequate backups
- Social engineering or phishing attacks
- Compromised exchange or custodian

**Mitigation:** Choose an appropriate custody model, implement multi-sig where possible, maintain encrypted backups in geographically separate locations, and establish clear access protocols.

### Regulatory Risk

The Australian regulatory landscape for digital assets continues to evolve:
- ATO tax treatment could change (CGT vs trading stock classification)
- ASIC may introduce new disclosure or licensing requirements
- International regulatory developments may impact Australian policy
- Banking relationships may be affected — some institutions are cautious about crypto-related entities

**Mitigation:** Monitor regulatory developments, engage specialist advisers, maintain detailed records, and build relationships with crypto-friendly banking partners.

### Reputational Risk

Depending on your stakeholders, a Bitcoin allocation may:
- Be seen as speculative or irresponsible
- Attract media scrutiny (especially for public companies and NFPs)
- Concern conservative board members or beneficiaries
- Create perception issues with donors or grant bodies (for NFPs)

**Mitigation:** Develop a clear communication strategy, frame the allocation within a broader treasury management policy, and ensure board or governance body approval is documented.

### Liquidity Risk

While Bitcoin is highly liquid globally, converting large positions to AUD may:
- Move the market (for very large positions)
- Take time through Australian exchanges
- Be restricted during exchange outages or bank processing delays

**Mitigation:** Use reputable exchanges with deep AUD/BTC liquidity, consider OTC desks for large trades, and maintain adequate AUD reserves for operational needs.

### Accounting and Tax Complexity

Bitcoin holdings create additional compliance requirements:
- Fair value measurement at reporting dates
- CGT event tracking for every disposal
- GST considerations (Bitcoin is not subject to GST in Australia as a form of payment)
- Potential complexity with existing accounting software
- Audit requirements for certain entity types

**Mitigation:** Engage a crypto-experienced accountant, use specialist tracking software, and maintain meticulous transaction records from day one.
