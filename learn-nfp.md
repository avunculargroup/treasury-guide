# Bitcoin Treasury — Not-for-Profit Organisations (NFP)

> **Disclaimer:** This content is educational only and does not constitute financial, legal, or tax advice. NFP boards and committees should consult qualified professionals before making any investment decisions.

---

## Overview

Not-for-profit (NFP) organisations in Australia encompass a broad range of entities: registered charities, community organisations, sporting clubs, religious organisations, professional associations, and more. The regulatory framework varies significantly depending on the type of NFP, whether it is a registered charity, and what state or territory it operates in.

Holding Bitcoin as a treasury asset is a relatively novel concept for NFPs, and it raises unique considerations around fiduciary duty, tax-exempt status, donor expectations, and public accountability.

## Types of NFP Entities

Understanding your entity type is essential, as the rules differ:

| Entity Type | Key Regulator | Governing Legislation |
|---|---|---|
| Registered Charity (company limited by guarantee) | ACNC, ASIC | ACNC Act, Corporations Act |
| Registered Charity (incorporated association) | ACNC, State/Territory regulator | ACNC Act, State Associations Act |
| Registered Charity (trust) | ACNC | ACNC Act, State Trustee Act |
| Non-charity NFP (company) | ASIC | Corporations Act |
| Non-charity NFP (incorporated association) | State/Territory regulator | State Associations Act |
| Unincorporated association | No formal regulator | Common law, association rules |

## Why NFPs Might Consider Bitcoin

### Preserving Purchasing Power of Reserves
Many NFPs hold significant cash reserves that lose purchasing power over time due to inflation. A modest Bitcoin allocation could serve as a hedge, helping to maintain the real value of reserves intended for future use.

### Diversification of Reserves
NFPs that hold reserves in bank deposits and term deposits are concentrated in Australian dollar cash equivalents. Adding a non-correlated asset like Bitcoin can reduce portfolio concentration.

### Accepting Bitcoin Donations
Some NFPs may receive Bitcoin donations from supporters. Understanding how to hold, manage, and convert these donations is increasingly relevant.

### Innovation and Mission Alignment
For NFPs whose mission relates to financial inclusion, technology, or economic empowerment, holding Bitcoin may align with their organisational purpose and demonstrate thought leadership.

## Fiduciary Duties and Governance

### Board Responsibilities

The board (or committee of management) of an NFP has fiduciary duties that directly affect any decision to hold Bitcoin:

**Duty of Care and Diligence**
Board members must exercise reasonable care and diligence in making investment decisions. For Bitcoin, this means:
- Researching and understanding the asset before investing
- Considering the risks, including volatility and potential total loss
- Seeking professional advice where the board lacks expertise
- Documenting the decision-making process

**Duty to Act in Good Faith and for a Proper Purpose**
The investment must serve the organisation's purposes. Board members must not use the investment for personal benefit or purposes unrelated to the NFP's objects.

**Duty to Not Misuse Position or Information**
Board members with personal cryptocurrency holdings must declare any conflict of interest and may need to recuse themselves from the decision.

### ACNC Governance Standards (for Registered Charities)

Registered charities must comply with ACNC Governance Standards:

- **Standard 1: Purposes and not-for-profit nature** — The charity must work towards its charitable purpose and must not distribute profits or assets to members
- **Standard 2: Accountability to members** — Members must be informed about the charity's activities, including investment decisions
- **Standard 3: Compliance with Australian laws** — The charity must comply with all relevant legislation
- **Standard 5: Duties of responsible persons** — Board members must exercise care and diligence, act in good faith, and not misuse their position

### Documenting the Decision

Any decision to invest in Bitcoin should be formally documented:

1. **Board paper** — A written proposal outlining the investment rationale, risks, proposed allocation, custody arrangements, and how the investment serves the organisation's purposes
2. **Board resolution** — A formal resolution passed at a board meeting, recorded in the minutes
3. **Risk assessment** — A documented assessment of the risks, including volatility, security, regulatory, and reputational risks
4. **Investment policy** — An updated investment policy that explicitly addresses digital assets
5. **Professional advice** — Records of any professional advice obtained (legal, financial, tax)

## Tax Considerations

### Income Tax Exemption

Most registered charities and certain other NFPs are exempt from income tax under Division 50 of the ITAA 1997. If the organisation is income tax exempt:

- Capital gains on Bitcoin disposals are not subject to CGT
- However, the exemption is conditional on the organisation continuing to meet the requirements for its exempt status
- If the ATO determines the Bitcoin investment is not consistent with the organisation's exempt purposes, the exemption could be at risk

### Non-Exempt NFPs

Some NFPs are not income tax exempt (e.g., certain sporting clubs, professional associations). For these entities:

- Bitcoin gains are subject to CGT at the company tax rate (if a company) or the applicable rate for the entity type
- The 50% CGT discount is not available to companies
- The small business entity concessions may apply depending on turnover

### GST

The GST treatment for NFPs is the same as for other entities:
- Sales of Bitcoin are GST-free
- Purchases of Bitcoin do not give rise to GST credits
- Supplies of goods or services in exchange for Bitcoin are subject to normal GST rules

### Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT)

If the NFP provides Bitcoin to employees as part of their remuneration, FBT may apply. NFP FBT concessions (capping and rebates) may reduce the FBT liability depending on the type of NFP.

## Receiving Bitcoin Donations

### Tax Implications for Donors

For donors to claim a tax deduction for a Bitcoin donation, the NFP must:
- Be a Deductible Gift Recipient (DGR)
- The donation must be a genuine gift (no material benefit provided to the donor)
- The ATO has not issued specific guidance on in-kind cryptocurrency donations to DGRs, so donors should seek their own tax advice

### Practical Considerations

When receiving Bitcoin donations:
- Establish a clear policy on whether the organisation accepts Bitcoin donations
- Set up a dedicated receiving wallet
- Convert to AUD promptly if the organisation's policy does not support holding Bitcoin
- Record the AUD market value at the time of receipt
- Issue a donation receipt if the NFP is a DGR
- Consider the AML/CTF implications of receiving Bitcoin from unknown sources

## Funding and Grant Compliance

### Grant Conditions

Many NFPs receive government grants or philanthropic funding with conditions on how the funds are used and invested. Before investing grant funds in Bitcoin:

- Review all grant agreements for investment restrictions
- Confirm with the grantor that the proposed investment is permitted
- Ensure Bitcoin holdings do not breach any acquittal or reporting requirements
- Maintain clear records showing which funds are invested in Bitcoin and which are held for grant purposes

### Donor Restrictions

Some donations are received with conditions (restricted funds). These funds must be used for the specified purpose. Investing restricted funds in Bitcoin without the donor's consent may breach the conditions and expose the organisation to legal liability.

## Risk Management

### Volatility Risk
Bitcoin's price can fluctuate significantly in short periods. For an NFP with ongoing operational funding needs, a large unrealised loss could impair the organisation's ability to fund its activities. Mitigation: limit the allocation to a small percentage of total reserves and only use unrestricted surplus funds.

### Reputational Risk
Public perception of Bitcoin varies. Donors, members, and the public may view a Bitcoin investment as irresponsible or speculative. Mitigation: communicate transparently about the investment rationale, allocation size, and risk management approach.

### Security Risk
Loss of Bitcoin through hacking, key loss, or exchange failure would directly reduce the organisation's assets. Mitigation: use institutional custody, implement strong security protocols, and consider whether insurance is available.

### Regulatory Risk
Changes to tax law, ACNC governance standards, or digital asset regulation could affect the organisation's ability to hold Bitcoin. Mitigation: monitor regulatory developments and maintain flexibility to divest if required.

## Getting Started: Key Steps

1. **Review your constitution** — Confirm the organisation's objects and investment powers permit Bitcoin investment
2. **Assess the regulatory framework** — Understand which regulators apply and what their requirements are
3. **Seek professional advice** — Engage a solicitor and tax adviser with NFP and digital asset experience
4. **Prepare a board paper** — Present a comprehensive proposal to the board covering rationale, risks, allocation, and governance
5. **Obtain board approval** — Pass a formal resolution and record it in the minutes
6. **Update your investment policy** — Explicitly address digital assets in the organisation's investment policy
7. **Set up custody and exchange accounts** — Establish secure infrastructure in the organisation's name
8. **Start small** — Make an initial small purchase to test the full process
9. **Report transparently** — Include Bitcoin holdings in financial reports and communicate with stakeholders

> NFPs hold a position of public trust. Any decision to invest in Bitcoin should be made with careful consideration of fiduciary duties, stakeholder expectations, and the organisation's core mission. Transparency and good governance are essential.

## Regulatory Context

> This section provides educational information about the regulatory environment for not-for-profit organisations. It does not constitute legal, financial, or tax advice. NFP boards should consult qualified professionals for advice specific to their organisation.

### Governing Legislation

#### Australian Charities and Not-for-profits Commission Act 2012 (ACNC Act)

The ACNC Act establishes the ACNC as the national regulator for registered charities. Key provisions relevant to Bitcoin holdings:

**Section 15-5 — Registration as a Charity**
To maintain registration, a charity must be not-for-profit, have exclusively charitable purposes, and comply with governance standards. Investment activities must not undermine the charitable status.

**Section 25-5 — Governance Standards**
Registered charities must comply with governance standards set by the Minister. The current standards include:

- **Governance Standard 1** — Purposes and not-for-profit nature: The charity must operate exclusively for its charitable purposes and not distribute profits to members. Bitcoin holdings must serve the charity's purposes (e.g., prudent management of reserves).
- **Governance Standard 2** — Accountability to members: Responsible persons must take reasonable steps to be accountable to the charity's members, including informing them about activities and financial position.
- **Governance Standard 3** — Compliance with Australian laws: The charity must not commit a serious offence or breach of relevant laws.
- **Governance Standard 5** — Duties of responsible persons: Board members must exercise their powers and discharge their duties with care and diligence, act in good faith in the best interests of the charity, not misuse their position, and manage conflicts of interest.

**Section 55-5 — Reporting Requirements**
Registered charities must submit an Annual Information Statement (AIS) to the ACNC. Charities classified as medium or large must also submit financial reports. Bitcoin holdings would be disclosed in the financial statements.

#### Corporations Act 2001

For NFPs structured as companies limited by guarantee (registered with ASIC):

**Section 181 — Duty of Care and Diligence**
Directors must exercise their powers and discharge their duties with the degree of care and diligence that a reasonable person would exercise. Investment in a volatile asset like Bitcoin requires demonstrable due diligence.

**Section 182 — No Improper Use of Position**
Directors must not use their position to gain an advantage for themselves or someone else, or to cause detriment to the company. Personal cryptocurrency interests must be disclosed and managed.

**Section 191 — Disclosure of Material Personal Interests**
Directors must disclose any material personal interest in a matter relating to the company's affairs. A director with personal Bitcoin holdings has a material interest in a board decision about the company investing in Bitcoin.

**Section 180 — Business Judgment Rule**
A director is taken to meet the duty of care and diligence if they make a business judgment in good faith for a proper purpose, do not have a material personal interest, inform themselves to the extent they reasonably believe is appropriate, and rationally believe the judgment is in the best interests of the corporation. Documenting the decision-making process for a Bitcoin investment is essential to rely on this protection.

#### State and Territory Associations Incorporation Acts

For NFPs structured as incorporated associations, the relevant state or territory Act governs the organisation's operations. Key provisions typically include:

- The committee must manage the association's affairs in accordance with the rules (constitution)
- Committee members owe duties of care, diligence, good faith, and to act for proper purposes
- The association's funds must be used to further its objects as stated in the rules
- Financial reporting requirements vary by state/territory and organisation size

#### State and Territory Charitable Fundraising Legislation

If the NFP fundraises from the public, state/territory fundraising legislation may apply:

| State/Territory | Key Legislation |
|---|---|
| NSW | Charitable Fundraising Act 1991 |
| VIC | Fundraising Act 1998 |
| QLD | Collections Act 1966 |
| SA | Collections for Charitable Purposes Act 1939 |
| WA | Charitable Collections Act 1946 |
| TAS | Collections for Charities Act 2001 |
| ACT | Charitable Collections Act 2003 |
| NT | Gaming Control Act 1993 (Part 8) |

These Acts may impose requirements on how donated funds are managed and invested. Some require that donated funds be kept in a specific type of account or used within specified timeframes.

#### State and Territory Trustee Acts

For NFPs structured as charitable trusts, the relevant state or territory Trustee Act governs the trustee's investment powers:

- Trustees must invest trust funds prudently (the "prudent person" rule)
- Modern Trustee Acts generally provide broad investment powers, subject to the duty to exercise care, skill, and diligence
- The trustee must consider the purposes of the trust, diversification, the nature of existing investments, the risk of capital loss, and the expected return
- Specific trust deed provisions may restrict or expand the trustee's investment powers

### Regulatory Bodies

#### Australian Charities and Not-for-profits Commission (ACNC)

The ACNC is the national regulator for registered charities. It:
- Registers and monitors charities
- Maintains the public Charity Register
- Provides guidance on governance and compliance
- Can issue compliance directions, warnings, and enforceable undertakings
- Can suspend or remove responsible persons
- Can revoke a charity's registration for serious non-compliance

**ACNC Commissioner's Discretion:**
The ACNC Commissioner has broad powers to investigate charities that may not be operating in accordance with their charitable purposes. An investment decision that appears inconsistent with the charity's objects could prompt an ACNC investigation.

#### Australian Taxation Office (ATO)

The ATO administers:
- Income tax exemptions for charities and other NFPs (Division 50 ITAA 1997)
- DGR endorsement and compliance
- GST obligations
- FBT concessions for NFPs
- ABN and TFN registration

**Division 50 — Tax-Exempt Entities:**
The following categories of NFP may be income tax exempt (subject to conditions):

| Category | ITAA 1997 Section | Key Condition |
|---|---|---|
| Charitable institutions | s50-5 | Must be a charity |
| Religious institutions | s50-5 | Must be a religious institution |
| Scientific institutions | s50-5 | Must be a scientific institution |
| Community service organisations | s50-10 | Must apply income for community service purposes |
| Sporting clubs | s50-45 | Must encourage a sport or sporting activity |
| Cultural organisations | s50-25 | Must promote the development of culture |

For tax-exempt NFPs, capital gains on Bitcoin are not assessable. However, the exemption is conditional on the organisation continuing to meet the requirements for its exempt category. If the ATO determines that the NFP is not operating exclusively for its exempt purpose, the exemption can be revoked.

#### ASIC

ASIC regulates NFPs that are companies limited by guarantee. ASIC's role includes:
- Registering the company and maintaining company records
- Administering directors' duties provisions of the Corporations Act
- Receiving annual financial reports (though ACNC-registered charities are generally exempt from ASIC reporting)

#### State and Territory Regulators

Each state and territory has regulators responsible for:
- Incorporated associations (e.g., NSW Fair Trading, Consumer Affairs Victoria)
- Charitable fundraising compliance
- Trust regulation (through the Attorney-General or equivalent)

### Tax Treatment

#### Income Tax Exempt NFPs

For tax-exempt NFPs:
- No income tax on Bitcoin capital gains
- No income tax on Bitcoin received as donations or income
- The exemption applies as long as the organisation maintains its exempt status
- The organisation must still maintain records and may need to report Bitcoin holdings in financial statements

#### Non-Exempt NFPs

For NFPs that are not income tax exempt:
- Bitcoin gains are subject to CGT or income tax depending on the classification (CGT asset vs. trading stock)
- Companies limited by guarantee pay tax at the company rate (25% for base rate entities or 30%)
- The 50% CGT discount is not available to companies
- Incorporated associations may be taxed differently depending on the relevant state legislation

#### Goods and Services Tax

- Sales of Bitcoin are GST-free (regardless of the NFP's GST registration status)
- NFPs with GST concessions (e.g., increasing the GST registration threshold to $150,000) should consider how Bitcoin transactions interact with their GST reporting
- If the NFP supplies goods or services in exchange for Bitcoin, the underlying supply is subject to normal GST rules

#### Fringe Benefits Tax

- If the NFP provides Bitcoin to employees or volunteers, FBT may apply
- NFP FBT concessions may apply:
  - Certain charities and hospitals are exempt from FBT up to a capping threshold ($30,000 per employee for public benevolent institutions)
  - Other NFPs may be eligible for an FBT rebate of 48%

### DGR-Specific Requirements

Organisations with Deductible Gift Recipient (DGR) endorsement must comply with additional requirements:

#### Fund Restrictions
- DGR funds must be used for the specific DGR purpose
- Investing DGR funds in Bitcoin must serve the DGR purpose (e.g., prudent management of reserves intended for the DGR activity)
- If DGR funds are lost through a Bitcoin investment, this could be viewed as a misapplication of DGR funds

#### Gift Conditions
- A gift must be voluntary and without material benefit to the donor
- If a donor gives Bitcoin to a DGR, the gift should be valued at market value at the time of the gift
- The DGR must issue a receipt for tax-deductible gifts over $2

#### Winding Up Provisions
- DGRs must have a clause requiring that, on winding up, remaining DGR assets are transferred to another DGR with similar purposes
- Bitcoin holdings at the time of winding up would need to be transferred or converted and transferred

### Financial Reporting Requirements

#### ACNC Reporting Thresholds

| Size | Annual Revenue | Reporting Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Small | Less than $500,000 | AIS only (no financial report) |
| Medium | $500,000 to $3 million | AIS plus reviewed or audited financial report |
| Large | Over $3 million | AIS plus audited financial report |

#### Accounting for Bitcoin

Under Australian Accounting Standards (applicable to medium and large charities):
- Bitcoin is generally classified as an intangible asset (AASB 138) or measured at fair value under AASB 13
- Fair value measurement at reporting date is required
- Gains and losses are recognised in the statement of comprehensive income
- Disclosure of the nature, carrying amount, and fair value methodology is required

#### Audit Considerations

For NFPs required to be audited:
- The auditor must be able to verify the existence and ownership of Bitcoin holdings
- The auditor must assess whether the investment is consistent with the organisation's objects and investment policy
- The auditor may include an emphasis of matter paragraph if Bitcoin holdings are material and represent a significant risk

### Compliance Framework

#### Board Decision Process

1. **Identify the proposal** — A board member or management presents the idea of investing in Bitcoin
2. **Declare conflicts of interest** — Any board member with personal cryptocurrency holdings must declare the conflict
3. **Seek professional advice** — Engage legal and financial advisers to assess the proposal
4. **Prepare a board paper** — Document the rationale, risks, allocation, governance, and compliance considerations
5. **Pass a resolution** — The board votes on the proposal and records the resolution in the minutes
6. **Implement with proper controls** — Set up accounts, custody, and record-keeping systems
7. **Report to stakeholders** — Communicate the decision to members and other stakeholders as appropriate

#### Ongoing Governance

- Include Bitcoin holdings in regular financial reporting to the board
- Review the investment at least annually or when material events occur
- Maintain comprehensive records for audit and regulatory purposes
- Monitor regulatory changes from the ACNC, ATO, and relevant state/territory regulators
- Be prepared to divest if regulatory or governance requirements change

> The ACNC publishes governance guides and resources specifically for charity responsible persons. NFP boards should familiarise themselves with these resources before making significant investment decisions.
