# Public Company Regulatory Context: Bitcoin Treasury Holdings

## Governing Legislation

### Corporations Act 2001

The primary legislation governing ASX-listed companies. Key provisions:

**Chapter 2D — Directors' Duties**
- s 180: Duty of care and diligence (includes business judgment rule)
- s 181: Duty to act in good faith and for a proper purpose
- s 182: No improper use of position
- s 183: No improper use of information
- s 191: Disclosure of material personal interests

**Chapter 6CA — Continuous Disclosure**
- s 674: Listed entities must comply with continuous disclosure provisions in the listing rules
- s 674A: Criminal liability for intentional or reckless contravention
- Civil penalty provisions for failure to disclose

**Chapter 2M — Financial Reporting**
- Companies must prepare financial reports in accordance with Australian Accounting Standards
- Half-yearly and annual reporting obligations
- Directors' declaration on compliance with accounting standards

### ASX Listing Rules

**Listing Rule 3.1 — Continuous Disclosure**
An entity must immediately tell ASX of information that a reasonable person would expect to have a material effect on the price or value of its securities. Exceptions under LR 3.1A apply in limited circumstances.

**Listing Rule 3.1B — False Market**
If ASX considers that there is or is likely to be a false market in the entity's securities, it may require disclosure even if exceptions would otherwise apply.

**Listing Rule 4.2A/4.3A — Periodic Reports**
Half-yearly and annual reports must include complete financial statements. Bitcoin holdings would be reported under the applicable accounting standard.

**Listing Rule 10.1 — Substantial Assets**
Transactions involving substantial assets with related parties require shareholder approval. If Bitcoin holdings become substantial (5%+ of equity interests), related party transactions involving Bitcoin may trigger this rule.

**Listing Rule 11.1 — Significant Change**
A listed entity must not make a significant change to the nature or scale of its activities without shareholder approval. Whether a Bitcoin treasury constitutes a "significant change" depends on its materiality relative to the company.

**Listing Rule 12.2 — Appropriate Structure**
ASX may form the view that the entity's operations or structure are not appropriate for a listed entity. This is unlikely for a modest Bitcoin treasury allocation but could be relevant if Bitcoin becomes the dominant asset.

### Australian Securities and Investments Commission Act 2001

ASIC has broad powers to investigate and take enforcement action for:
- Market misconduct (insider trading, market manipulation)
- Misleading or deceptive conduct in relation to financial products
- Failure to comply with continuous disclosure obligations
- Breach of directors' duties

### Income Tax Assessment Act 1997

- Division 104: CGT events triggered by Bitcoin disposals
- Division 70: Trading stock provisions (if Bitcoin is held as trading stock)
- Division 820: Thin capitalisation rules (for multinational groups)
- Division 815: Transfer pricing rules (for related party cross-border transactions)

## Regulatory Bodies

### ASIC
ASIC regulates public companies and the securities market. Relevant functions:
- Monitoring continuous disclosure compliance
- Investigating breaches of directors' duties
- Reviewing financial reports for compliance with accounting standards
- Taking enforcement action for market misconduct
- Issuing regulatory guides and class orders

### ASX
ASX operates the securities market and administers the Listing Rules:
- Monitoring compliance with continuous disclosure obligations
- Issuing price queries when unusual trading activity occurs
- Reviewing material announcements before release
- Granting or withholding trading halts for material announcements

### ATO
The ATO administers tax obligations:
- Corporate income tax on Bitcoin gains
- Franking account implications
- Transfer pricing compliance for multinational groups
- Activity statement obligations

## Accounting Standards

### AASB 138 — Intangible Assets
Bitcoin is typically classified as an intangible asset with an indefinite useful life. Key requirements:
- Initial recognition at cost (purchase price plus directly attributable costs)
- Subsequent measurement at fair value (if revaluation model is adopted) or cost less impairment
- Fair value changes recognised in other comprehensive income (revaluation model) or impairment recognised in profit or loss (cost model)

### AASB 13 — Fair Value Measurement
Fair value of Bitcoin is determined using:
- Level 1 inputs: Quoted prices in active markets (exchange prices)
- The fair value hierarchy requires disclosure of the level of inputs used
- For large holdings, consideration of whether the quoted price reflects the price achievable for the full position

### AASB 7 / AASB 132 — Financial Instruments
If Bitcoin is classified as a financial asset (less common but possible under some interpretations):
- Detailed risk disclosures are required
- Sensitivity analysis for market risk
- Liquidity risk disclosures

### AASB 101 — Presentation of Financial Statements
- Bitcoin holdings should be separately disclosed if material
- Classification as current or non-current depends on the entity's intention (hold >12 months = non-current)
- Significant accounting policy disclosures required

## Compliance Checklist for Public Companies

### Pre-Decision
- [ ] Commission independent analysis of Bitcoin treasury risks and opportunities
- [ ] Obtain legal advice on ASX disclosure obligations
- [ ] Assess whether the strategy constitutes a "significant change" under LR 11.1
- [ ] Identify and manage director conflicts of interest
- [ ] Brief the company's external auditor
- [ ] Brief the company secretary on disclosure requirements

### Board Approval
- [ ] Prepare a comprehensive board paper covering rationale, risks, allocation, custody, and governance
- [ ] Obtain board approval via formal resolution
- [ ] Record detailed minutes of the discussion and decision
- [ ] Establish delegated authorities for Bitcoin transactions

### Disclosure
- [ ] Prepare ASX announcement (consult with ASX Listings if uncertain about disclosure requirements)
- [ ] Request trading halt if necessary during announcement preparation
- [ ] Brief major shareholders, broker, and analysts
- [ ] Prepare investor relations materials and FAQs
- [ ] Update the company's website with relevant information

### Implementation
- [ ] Engage an institutional custody provider
- [ ] Open accounts with AUSTRAC-registered exchanges
- [ ] Establish internal controls and approval workflows
- [ ] Set up accounting processes for fair value measurement
- [ ] Update the risk management framework
- [ ] Brief the external auditor on custody arrangements and verification procedures

### Ongoing
- [ ] Disclose material Bitcoin transactions to ASX as they occur
- [ ] Include Bitcoin holdings in half-yearly and annual financial reports
- [ ] Report to the board at least quarterly on Bitcoin holdings and unrealised gains/losses
- [ ] Review the Bitcoin treasury policy annually
- [ ] Monitor regulatory developments from ASIC, ASX, and ATO
- [ ] Assess shareholder sentiment at AGM and through ongoing engagement
